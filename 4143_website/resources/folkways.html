Why This Source

A wonderful resource provided by the Smithsonian, with an educational mission.

Summary

Smithsonian Folkways Recordings is the nonprofit record label of the Smithsonian Institution. The Young People's Records (YPR) collection is part of this archive, originating as an educational tool designed to be less commercial than mainstream children's media.

The label is an exceptional resource for folk music because it provides access to authentic primary sources. It hosts thousands of recordings from around the world, each accompanied by educational liner notes that detail the music's cultural and historical context. Smithsonian Folkways also offers ready-to-use lesson plans for educators.

I am highlighting one lesson plan, "Protest Songs: A Musical Introduction," designed by Amy R. Kenny for grades 6-8. For my Classroom Activities, I will adapt it for the elementary level.

Critical Analysis

Normalization

The YPR collection explicitly resisted the commercial, profit-driven model of children's entertainment. By prioritizing educational and cultural content over marketability, it challenged the normalization of consumerism in childhood and presented an alternative model where art and learning were not solely commodities.

Omitted Voices/Stories/Perspectives

As an archive, Smithsonian Folkways actively works against cultural erasure by preserving and promoting the music of marginalized, indigenous, and minority communities worldwide.

Windows and Mirrors

The vast global collection provides students with both "windows" into diverse cultures and "mirrors" for students to see their own heritage.

Link from Past to Present

Continuing to release new material, Folkways connects the past with its vast archive with the present.

Protest Songs Lesson

Class activity

Summary

Using the Protest Songs: A Musical Introduction lesson plan designed by Amy R Kenny (n.d.).

This lesson plan introduces three songs to the students, two of which they would be familiar from other lessons-“This Land is Your Land” (Guthrie, 1951) and “We Shall Overcome” (Seeger, 1963)--and two that would be new--“Turn, Turn, Turn” (Seeger, 1962) and “We Are Soldiers in the Army” (The Freedom Singers, 2010)

The original lesson plan is pretty straight forward, involving students evaluating and discussing the music and history.

This can be adapted into snowball, where students write down their ideas about the songs, or fishbowl, where they can discuss the songs and history of folk and protest.
