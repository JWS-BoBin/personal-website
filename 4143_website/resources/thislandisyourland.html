Why This Source

The widely recognized song has a rich, interesting history.

This allows students to reconsider two songs they may have taken for granted: "This Land Is Your Land" by Woody Guthrie and "God Bless America" by Irvine Berlin.

Summary

Written in 1940 as a response to "God Bless America," which Guthrie couldn't get away from due to constant radio play.

Guthrie felt the lyics to "God Bless America" were too patriotic and sappy, divorced from the tough reality many Americans experienced during the Great Depression.

Originally written with an anti-capitalist bend with the lyrics:
As I went walking I saw a sign there
And on the sign it said “No Trespassing.”
But on the other side it didn't say nothing,
That side was made for you and me.

In the shadow of the steeple I saw my people,
By the relief office I seen my people;
As they stood there hungry, I stood there asking
Is this land made for you and me?
 (Kennedy Center, n.d.)

He dropped those lyrics at some point, but the final version still resonates with the listener.

Official lyrics posted in the article:
This land is your land, this land is my land
From California to the New York island; From the red wood forest to the Gulf Stream waters
This land was made for you and me.

As I was walking that ribbon of highway,
I saw above me that endless skyway:
I saw below me that golden valley:
This land was made for you and me.

I've roamed and rambled and I followed my footsteps
To the sparkling sands of her diamond deserts;
And all around me a voice was sounding:
This land was made for you and me.

When the sun came shining, and I was strolling,
And the wheat fields waving and the dust clouds rolling,
As the fog was lifting a voice was chanting:
This land was made for you and me.

As I went walking I saw a sign there
And on the sign it said "No Trespassing."

In the squares of the city, in the shadow of the steeple,

Nobody living can ever stop me,
As I go walking that freedom highway;
Nobody living can ever make me turn back
This land was made for you and me.
(Kennedy Center, n.d.)

Critical Analysis

Idealization

The song directly responds to and challenges the sentimental, shallow patriotism of "God Bless America," which ignored the harsh realities many Americans experienced during the Great Depression.

Resistance

The omitted verses explicitly critique capitalism and private property. Even without them, the song's framing of land as a collective right questions exclusive ownership and inequality.

What does it mean to be American?

The song poses a fundamental question: What does it mean to belong here? Guthrie shifts the focus from a nation "blessed by God" (passive, top-down) to a land "made for you and me" (inclusive, bottom up).

Reclaiming/Reframing

Guthrie's work is a deliberate effort to reclaim the concept of America from a commercial, patriotic mainstream.

Art Time!

Class activity

Summary

There is a strong visual element to "This Land Is Your Land" (Guthrie, 1951).

Students listen to the song and read the lyrics. They then do art, probably drawing or painting, of the visuals the song inspires in them.
