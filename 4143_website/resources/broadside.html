Why This Source

Founded by Agnes Cunningham and Gordon Friesen in 1962, Broadside was a magazine that covered the folk revival. (Dicaire, 2011)

Broadside is an amazing primary source that provides contemporary context, songs and plenty of options for class interaction.

Issues, or sections of issues, can be selected for curated activities, making it highly adaptable for educational use.

Summary

Broadside was a popular magazine created within and centered around the folk music scene of the 1960s.

It was a unique combination of songbook, poetry, photocopied news articles, and other elements such as the occasional cartoon.

It featured contributions from major folk and protest music figures, including Bob Dylan, Phil Ochs, Pete Seeger, and Nina Simone.

Critical Analysis

Omitted stories

As a document centered on the folk scene's perspective, Broadside provides a specific look into the political counterculture of the 1960s. Its focus necessarily omits or provides a counter-narrative to the stories promoted by contemporary government and corporate media, offering a curated view of the era's struggles and triumphs from the ground level.

Power & resistance

Broadside was a vocal critic of the political and social establishment, using song and commentary as direct tools of protest. Simultaneously, it functioned as an empowering platform that circumvented mainstream media, giving a direct voice and promotional power to songwriters whose work challenged the status quo.

Solidarity

The magazine served as a vital nexus for the folk community, fostering a shared identity and political consciousness. By publishing lyrics, stories, and news, it created a common text and purpose that brought artists and activists together across the country.

Joy as Resistance

Beyond its protest material, the magazine was a celebration of folk music and folk culture. This celebration—through sharing songs, cartoons, and poetry—was itself an act of resistance, affirming the value and vitality of a culture that stood outside the commercial mainstream.
